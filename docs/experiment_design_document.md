# Experiment Design Document
## 20% First-Order Promo Discount A/B Test

**Document version:** 1.0  
**Author:** Akash Singh  
**Date:** June 2026  
**Status:** Approved for analysis  

---

## 1. Background and Business Context

Food delivery platforms like DoorDash, Instacart, and Uber Eats routinely offer
first-order discounts to attract new customers. A 20% discount on a $35 average
order costs the platform $7 per acquired user in direct margin. The core business
question is whether that $7 investment generates enough repeat order behavior to
be profitable over a 90-day window.

The marketing team has proposed scaling the 20% first-order discount across all
new user acquisition channels. Before committing to a platform-wide rollout, the
analytics team was asked to evaluate the true incremental impact of the promotion
on customer lifetime behavior — not just first-order conversion.

This document defines the experiment scope, hypotheses, metrics, and decision
criteria before any data is analyzed, following standard pre-registration practice
to prevent p-hacking and post-hoc rationalization of results.

---

## 2. Business Question

> Does offering a 20% discount on a new user's first order increase their
> probability of placing a second order within 30, 60, and 90 days — and does
> the incremental revenue generated by that repeat behavior exceed the cost of
> the discount?

---

## 3. Hypotheses

### Null hypothesis (H0)
The 20% first-order discount has no statistically significant effect on the
30-day reorder rate of new users compared to new users who received no discount.

### Alternative hypothesis (H1)
New users who received a 20% first-order discount have a statistically
significantly higher 30-day reorder rate than new users who received no discount.

### Direction
This is a one-tailed test. We are testing specifically whether the promo
increases reorder rate, not whether it changes it in either direction. A decrease
in reorder rate would be interpreted as a failed promotion regardless.

---

## 4. Experiment Setup

### Design type
Randomized controlled experiment (A/B test). Users are randomly assigned to one
of two groups at the point of first app session, before any order is placed.

### Groups

| Group | Label | Treatment | Expected size |
|---|---|---|---|
| Control | A | No discount — standard first-order experience | 50% of new users |
| Treatment | B | 20% discount applied automatically to first order | 50% of new users |

### Randomization unit
Individual user (user_id). Randomization at user level prevents the same user
from appearing in both groups across sessions or devices.

### Experiment window
- Enrollment period: 30 days (new users only)
- Observation period: 90 days post first order
- Total experiment duration: 120 days

### Exclusion criteria
- Users who have placed any prior order on the platform (returning users excluded)
- Users who abandon before completing a first order in either group
- Users flagged for duplicate accounts or fraud signals

---

## 5. Metrics

### Primary metric
**30-day reorder rate** — the proportion of users in each group who place at
least one additional order within 30 days of their first order.

This is the primary decision metric. If this metric does not show a statistically
significant improvement in the treatment group, the promotion will not be
recommended for rollout regardless of other metrics.

### Secondary metrics
**60-day reorder rate** — same definition, extended window. Measures whether the
promo effect persists beyond the initial engagement period.

**90-day reorder rate** — full observation window. Captures the long-term loyalty
signal, which is the true revenue driver.

**Average order value (AOV) per user** — measures whether promo users order
lower-value items to maximize the discount benefit. A meaningful drop in AOV
would reduce the net revenue per reorder.

**Number of orders placed in 90 days** — total order volume per user across the
full window, not just binary reorder yes/no.

### Guardrail metrics
These metrics must not degrade significantly in the treatment group. If any
guardrail is violated, the experiment is flagged for review even if the primary
metric shows improvement.

**Net revenue per user at 90 days** — total revenue from all orders minus the $7
discount cost for treatment group users. If net revenue per treatment user does
not exceed net revenue per control user, the promotion destroys value.

**Refund and cancellation rate** — promo users should not show a meaningfully
higher refund or cancellation rate, which would indicate low-quality engagement
driven purely by the discount incentive.

**Customer support contact rate** — a spike in the treatment group would suggest
the promo experience introduced friction or unmet expectations.

---

## 6. Statistical Parameters

### Significance level
Alpha = 0.05 (95% confidence). We will reject the null hypothesis only if the
p-value falls below 0.05.

### Statistical power
Power = 0.80 (80%). There is an 80% probability that the test will detect a true
effect if one exists at the specified minimum detectable effect size.

### Minimum detectable effect (MDE)
The smallest improvement in reorder rate that the business considers meaningful
is 5 percentage points. If the true treatment effect is smaller than 5pp, it does
not justify the $7 per-user discount cost at current margins.

Baseline reorder rate assumption (from historical data): 28%  
MDE: 5 percentage points (target treatment reorder rate: 33%)

### Required sample size
Based on the parameters above, the minimum required sample size is calculated
using a two-proportion z-test power analysis:

- Baseline conversion rate (p1): 0.28
- Target treatment rate (p2): 0.33
- Alpha: 0.05 (one-tailed)
- Power: 0.80
- Required users per group: calculated in Phase 3 (power analysis notebook)

### Multiple testing correction
Secondary metrics are tested at alpha = 0.10 to reduce false negatives given
lower business stakes. No Bonferroni correction is applied to secondary metrics
because they are exploratory, not decision-driving.

---

## 7. Statistical Tests to Be Applied

| Metric | Test | Rationale |
|---|---|---|
| 30/60/90-day reorder rate | Two-proportion z-test | Binary outcome, large sample |
| Average order value distribution | Mann-Whitney U test | Non-normal distribution, right-skewed |
| Reorder behavior by user segment | Chi-square test | Categorical comparison across segments |
| Net revenue per user | Mann-Whitney U test | Right-skewed monetary distribution |

---

## 8. Segmentation Plan

Results will be cut by the following dimensions to identify whether the promo
effect is uniform or concentrated in specific user segments:

- Order frequency tier (users who reordered 1x vs. 2x vs. 3x+ in 90 days)
- Day-of-week of first order (weekday vs. weekend behavior differs)
- Department mix of first order (produce, snacks, beverages, frozen, etc.)
- Number of distinct departments ordered across 90-day window

These cuts are exploratory. No multiplicity correction is applied. Findings from
segmentation inform follow-up experiment design, not the primary Go/No-Go call.

---

## 9. Decision Criteria

### Go — recommend rollout
All three conditions must be met:

1. 30-day reorder rate in treatment group is at least 5 percentage points higher
   than control (p < 0.05, one-tailed z-test)
2. Net revenue per user at 90 days is positive for the treatment group after
   subtracting the $7 discount cost
3. No guardrail metric (refund rate, cancellation rate, support contact rate)
   shows a statistically significant degradation in the treatment group

### Conditional go — recommend rollout with modifications
Primary metric is significant but a guardrail is degraded:

- Deploy with an average order value floor of $25 (discount applies only if
  first order exceeds $25, protecting against discount-maximizing behavior)
- Re-evaluate refund and cancellation rates at 30-day mark before full rollout

### No-Go — do not recommend rollout
Any of the following conditions:

1. 30-day reorder rate difference is not statistically significant (p >= 0.05)
2. Net revenue per treatment user at 90 days is negative after discount cost
3. AOV in treatment group is more than 15% lower than control group

---

## 10. Risks and Limitations

**Novelty effect:** Users in the treatment group may reorder more frequently in
the first 30 days simply because the discount created a positive first impression
that fades. The 60 and 90-day metrics are designed to test whether the effect
persists beyond this window.

**Selection bias in simulation:** The Instacart dataset used in this analysis
does not contain a real controlled experiment. Experiment group assignment is
simulated by randomly splitting historical users. This introduces the limitation
that we cannot fully control for all user-level confounders that a real
randomized experiment would control for. Results are interpreted as directional
estimates, not causal claims.

**Cannibalization:** If the platform runs other concurrent promotions, the
treatment effect could be diluted or amplified. This analysis assumes no
concurrent promotional interference.

**Dataset scope:** The Instacart Market Basket Analysis dataset covers grocery
delivery, not restaurant delivery. AOV and reorder rate baselines may differ from
a restaurant-focused platform like DoorDash or Uber Eats. The analytical
methodology is fully transferable; the specific numbers would require calibration
against platform-specific data.
